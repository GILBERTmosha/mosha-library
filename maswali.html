Question 9: Explain how decision-making is carried out in Co-operatives

Key Concept: Co-operative decision-making is based on democratic principles where members have equal power regardless of their financial contribution.
1
Democratic Member Control
Decision-making is fundamentally democratic. Co-operatives are controlled by their members who actively participate in setting policies and making key decisions.
2
One Member, One Vote Principle
This is the core of co-operative governance. Regardless of the amount of capital or shares a member contributes, each person has exactly one vote, ensuring equality.
3
The General Meeting (Supreme Authority)
Major decisions are made at the Annual General Meeting (AGM) or Special General Meetings. This is where members exercise their ultimate power to approve budgets and plans.
4
Election of the Board of Directors
Members make decisions by electing a Board of Directors from among themselves. This board acts as the representative body to oversee the co-operative's interests.
5
Policy Formulation by the Board
The Board of Directors is responsible for making strategic and policy-level decisions. They set the long-term goals and ensure the co-operative stays true to its mission.
6
Delegation to Management
For daily operations, decisions are delegated to professional managers or staff. This ensures that technical and routine tasks are handled efficiently by experts.
7
Accountability to Members
Decision-making includes a feedback loop. The Board and Management must report their actions and results back to the members for review and approval.
8
Use of Specialized Committees
Many co-operatives form sub-committees (e.g., Audit or Education committees) to research specific issues and provide recommendations before a final decision is made.
9
Compliance with Bylaws and Laws
All decisions must be made within the framework of the co-operative's own bylaws and the national laws governing co-operative societies.
10
Majority Rule
Most decisions are finalized through a majority vote. For critical issues like merging or dissolving the co-operative, a higher threshold (like a two-thirds majority) is often required.
